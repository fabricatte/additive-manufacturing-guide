Nylon — or polyamide (PA) — is the workhorse material of industrial 3D printing. It's strong, flexible, chemically resistant, and available across every major powder bed fusion platform. But walk into any additive manufacturing bureau and you'll quickly discover that "nylon" isn't one material — it's a family of polymers with meaningfully different properties, costs, and manufacturing trade-offs.

PA6, PA11, and PA12 are the three grades that dominate production 3D printing. Choosing the wrong one can mean parts that warp in service, margins that evaporate, or mechanical performance that falls short. This guide breaks down exactly how these three polyamides compare so you can match the right nylon to your application.

## What Makes Nylon So Popular for Additive Manufacturing?

Before diving into the differences between PA grades, it's worth understanding why nylon dominates industrial 3D printing in the first place.

Nylon offers a rare combination of properties that few other polymers can match:

- **High strength-to-weight ratio** — nylon parts are lightweight yet mechanically robust
- **Excellent fatigue resistance** — parts survive repeated loading cycles without cracking
- **Chemical resistance** — nylon resists oils, greases, fuels, and many solvents
- **Low friction coefficient** — ideal for moving parts, gears, and sliding mechanisms
- **Good thermal stability** — service temperatures well above commodity plastics like PLA or ABS
- **Excellent powder recyclability** — unsintered powder can be reused, keeping material costs manageable

These properties make nylon the default choice for functional prototypes, end-use production parts, and everything in between. The question isn't whether to use nylon — it's which nylon to use.

## PA12: The Industry Standard

PA12 (Nylon 12) is by far the most widely used polyamide in 3D printing. If someone says they're printing in "nylon" without specifying a grade, they almost certainly mean PA12. It's the default material on HP Multi Jet Fusion (MJF) systems, EOS SLS machines, and most other powder bed platforms.

### Key Properties of PA12

| Property | Typical Value |
|---|---|
| Tensile Strength | 48–50 MPa |
| Elongation at Break | 15–20% |
| Heat Deflection Temp | 175–185°C (at 0.45 MPa) |
| Density | 1.01 g/cm³ |
| Moisture Absorption | 0.5–1.5% |
| Cost (per kg powder) | $50–80 |

### Why Manufacturers Choose PA12

PA12's dominance comes down to **processability and predictability**. It has the widest sintering window of any polyamide, meaning it's forgiving during printing — fewer warps, fewer curls, fewer failed builds. The powder ages gracefully, allowing refresh ratios of 50/50 or even 70/30 (used/virgin) without significant property loss.

For production environments where uptime and yield matter more than peak mechanical performance, PA12 is hard to beat. It delivers good-enough properties across nearly every metric while keeping scrap rates low and material costs manageable.

### Best Applications for PA12

- Consumer product housings and enclosures
- Snap-fit assemblies and clips
- Ducting and airflow components
- General-purpose functional prototypes
- Low-stress structural components
- Jigs, fixtures, and assembly aids

### PA12's Limitations

PA12's weakness is that it's the least mechanically impressive of the three grades. Its elongation at break is lower than PA11, its tensile strength is lower than PA6, and its chemical resistance — while good — doesn't match PA11's performance against hydrocarbons. For applications where parts face sustained mechanical loads or harsh chemical environments, the other grades pull ahead.

## PA11: The Sustainable Performance Option

PA11 (Nylon 11) is derived from castor oil — a renewable, plant-based feedstock — making it the most environmentally friendly option among the three grades. But sustainability isn't its only selling point. PA11 offers mechanical properties that PA12 simply can't match, particularly in ductility and impact resistance.

### Key Properties of PA11

| Property | Typical Value |
|---|---|
| Tensile Strength | 46–52 MPa |
| Elongation at Break | 30–40% |
| Heat Deflection Temp | 180–185°C (at 0.45 MPa) |
| Density | 1.03 g/cm³ |
| Moisture Absorption | 1.0–1.8% |
| Cost (per kg powder) | $80–120 |

### Why PA11 Stands Out

The headline number for PA11 is **elongation at break: 30–40%**, roughly double that of PA12. This means PA11 parts can bend, flex, and absorb impact energy without fracturing. In practice, this translates to parts that survive drops, vibration, and repeated mechanical cycling far better than their PA12 counterparts.

PA11 also excels in chemical resistance, particularly against hydrocarbons, alcohols, and ketones. This makes it the preferred choice for parts exposed to fuels, lubricants, or aggressive cleaning chemicals.

Its bio-based origin is increasingly relevant for manufacturers with sustainability mandates. PA11's carbon footprint is roughly 40% lower than petroleum-derived PA12, a difference that matters when large OEMs audit their supply chains.

### Best Applications for PA11

- Automotive under-hood components and fuel system parts
- Sports equipment and protective gear
- Prosthetics and orthotics requiring flexibility
- Snap-fits and living hinges that need repeated cycling
- Parts exposed to fuels, oils, or solvents
- Applications with sustainability or bio-sourcing requirements

### PA11's Trade-Offs

PA11 costs 50–80% more than PA12 per kilogram, and it absorbs more moisture from the atmosphere. Moisture absorption affects dimensional stability and mechanical properties — parts that sit in humid environments will swell slightly and lose some stiffness over time. Proper dry storage and conditioning protocols are essential for consistent PA11 production.

The sintering window is also narrower than PA12, making it slightly more demanding to process. Build failures and curling are more common, especially for operators who haven't dialed in their parameters.

## PA6: Maximum Mechanical Performance

PA6 (Nylon 6) is the high-performance option — the grade you reach for when parts need to withstand serious mechanical loads, high temperatures, or sustained stress. It's less commonly available on 3D printing platforms than PA12 or PA11, but its properties make it irreplaceable for demanding engineering applications.

### Key Properties of PA6

| Property | Typical Value |
|---|---|
| Tensile Strength | 70–85 MPa |
| Elongation at Break | 15–25% |
| Heat Deflection Temp | 200–220°C (at 0.45 MPa) |
| Density | 1.14 g/cm³ |
| Moisture Absorption | 2.5–3.5% |
| Cost (per kg powder) | $100–150 |

### Why PA6 Delivers Superior Strength

PA6's tensile strength — 70–85 MPa — is roughly **60–70% higher than PA12**. This isn't a marginal improvement; it's a different class of material. PA6 also offers significantly higher stiffness (Young's modulus around 2.5–3.5 GPa vs. 1.6–1.8 GPa for PA12) and better creep resistance under sustained loads.

The heat deflection temperature exceeding 200°C means PA6 parts maintain structural integrity in high-temperature environments where PA12 would soften and deform. For under-hood automotive, industrial equipment, and aerospace applications, this thermal headroom is critical.

### Best Applications for PA6

- Structural aerospace and automotive components
- High-load bearing brackets and mounts
- Gears and wear-resistant mechanical parts
- Electrical connectors and housings
- Components operating above 150°C
- Metal replacement applications

### PA6's Challenges

PA6 is the most difficult polyamide to 3D print. Its high melting point (around 220°C vs. 178°C for PA12) requires more energy input, and its narrow sintering window demands precise temperature control. Warping and curling are more pronounced, and build success rates are lower than PA12 — especially for large or complex geometries.

Moisture absorption is the most significant practical challenge. At 2.5–3.5%, PA6 absorbs substantially more water than PA11 or PA12. This means parts change dimensions and lose stiffness in humid environments unless they're sealed or coated. Storage, handling, and conditioning add complexity and cost to the production workflow.

Fewer SLS and MJF platforms officially support PA6 powder, limiting your options for service providers and in-house equipment.

## How to Choose the Right Nylon for Your Application

**Choose PA12 when:**
- You need reliable, cost-effective production at scale
- Parts don't face extreme mechanical loads or temperatures
- Processability and yield are priorities
- You're running MJF or SLS with standard configurations

**Choose PA11 when:**
- Parts need high ductility, impact resistance, or fatigue life
- Chemical exposure to hydrocarbons or solvents is expected
- Sustainability and bio-sourcing matter to your supply chain
- Parts include living hinges or snap-fits with repeated cycling

**Choose PA6 when:**
- Maximum tensile strength and stiffness are non-negotiable
- Parts operate at elevated temperatures (above 150°C)
- You're replacing metal components and need the highest-performing polymer
- You have the process expertise to manage its narrow sintering window

## The Blended Approach: Filled and Modified Nylons

Beyond the base grades, manufacturers increasingly use filled variants — glass-bead PA12, carbon-fiber PA11, mineral-filled PA6 — that modify specific properties. Glass-bead-filled PA12 (PA12 GB) offers significantly higher stiffness than neat PA12 at a modest cost premium, making it popular for dimensional stability in large parts. Carbon-fiber-filled nylons push stiffness and thermal performance even further, approaching aluminum in specific stiffness.

These filled variants add another dimension to material selection. If neat PA12 almost meets your requirements but falls short on stiffness, PA12 GB might solve the problem without jumping to the cost and complexity of PA6.
